# Brown Biotech Publications Drafts

## 1) Research brief
**Title:** Peptide-service briefs should start with the decision, not the method

**One-line takeaway:**
The fastest way to make a biotech service feel premium is to lead with the decision the client needs to make.

**What was done:**
We reframed the first touchpoint around the decision gap, then used the brief to scope the problem, the route, and the owner.

**Why it matters:**
Clients do not buy raw effort. They buy clarity, sequencing, and a clean next move.

**Next step:**
Turn this into the default homepage and intake language for peptide-service.

## 2) Case note
**Title:** One request came in, one owner was assigned, and the handoff stayed clean

**One-line takeaway:**
A good intake system should reduce ambiguity before it reaches the team.

**What was done:**
The request was triaged, routed to the right lane, and returned as a receipt with owner and next action.

**Why it matters:**
This lowers follow-up friction, reduces internal back-and-forth, and makes the system easier to trust.

**Next step:**
Capture more of these as short case notes rather than long internal memos.

## 3) Methods note
**Title:** Brown Biotech uses one brief, one owner, one next action

**One-line takeaway:**
The operating model is intentionally simple so the work stays executable.

**What was done:**
The same routing rule was aligned across the website, HQ, intake, and review docs.

**Why it matters:**
When the language matches across surfaces, the business feels coherent and easier to act on.

**Next step:**
Keep the naming stable and publish only what reinforces the system.

## Publish-ready format
- Title
- One-line takeaway
- What was done
- Why it matters
- Next step

## Rules
- Keep each piece short.
- Tie each piece to one decision.
- Do not publish anything that weakens approval gates.
- Prefer proof over promotion.
